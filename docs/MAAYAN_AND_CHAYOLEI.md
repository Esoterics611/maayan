# maayan and Chayolei Beis Dovid — how they fit

*An overview for supporters*

---

## Two complementary projects

Two separate projects, built alongside each other. One organizes the production of
Torah scholarship and rewards it; the other keeps what's produced and makes it
searchable. Here is what each does, and what is specific about joining them.

---

## What Chayolei does

A network organized as a DAO. Local yeshivot and kollelim connect into it. A mashpia
opens a *mivtza* (campaign) and breaks it into *masimot* (assignments) — a source
analysis, a chapter draft, a paper. Talmidim submit, peer-review each other's work,
and the mashpia approves.

The business side runs on an internal token, **TMIM**: for verified contribution a
talmid earns tokens and rises in rank, and rank sets their voting power and earning
multiplier. Supporters donate to the treasury; talmidim redeem what they've earned —
for stipends, for project funding (printing a sefer, hosting a farbrengen), or for
chessed. Allocations are voted on, and everything is recorded and transparent.

---

## What maayan does

A retrieval system that runs locally. For any question it finds the passages that
bear on it and answers strictly from them, with citations — or refuses when the
sources don't support an answer. The refusal is enforced in code, not merely
requested of the model, so it can be trusted not to fabricate.

On top of that it captures the scholar's work: a correction, a connection, or a
*seed* — a framework together with a direction for developing it. The system develops
the seed against the actual sources, the scholar approves, and the result becomes
searchable knowledge carrying its full provenance (who wrote it, which seed it grew
from, which sources it was grounded in). Reasoning accumulates instead of
evaporating.

---

## What is unique about joining them

The two line up at several specific points, because each was built around the same
underlying commitments.

- **One contribution serves both systems.** In Chayolei it is a rewarded unit of
  work; in maayan it is a permanent piece of a searchable corpus. One thing done,
  counted twice.

- **One gate, two jobs.** The peer-and-mashpia review already in Chayolei is also
  maayan's approval gate into the corpus. The same check that earns a contribution
  its reward is what admits it into the memory.

- **One identity, two uses.** A talmid's identity and rank in Chayolei are the
  provenance maayan attaches to every piece of knowledge. One side needs the identity
  for reward accounting; the other needs it for source credibility.

- **Grounding gives the reward something to stand on.** maayan defines what a
  high-value contribution actually is: not "a paper was submitted," but "grounded,
  cited knowledge that passed review and the whole network can retrieve."

- **One corpus across every makom.** maayan keeps a single attributed body of
  knowledge, so a kollel's work in one city compounds with a yeshiva's in another.

---

## What it looks like together

A mashpia opens a mivtza to develop a theme in a body of chassidus. A talmid takes a
masimah, works inside maayan — asks, sees cited sources, plants a seed. maayan
develops it against the sources and cites them, or reports honestly that the text
doesn't support it.

The work goes through Chayolei's normal review. Once approved, it does two things at
once: the talmid earns the reward and rank in Chayolei, and the contribution becomes
permanent, attributed corpus in maayan — which the next talmid, in another makom,
will find when they ask.

---

## Where things stand

These are two distinct projects today, with no integration built between them yet.
This document describes a fit, not a finished bridge. Each side would need a little:

- **maayan** — to accept an external identity and approval signal, so Chayolei's
  reviewer is the one who approves a contribution into the corpus.

- **Chayolei** — to treat a maayan contribution as a recognized form of masimah.

Neither is a large leap: both were built on the same two assumptions — that a
contribution must be attributed to an accountable person, and that it must be
reviewed before it counts. That shared ground is what makes the fit natural rather
than forced.
